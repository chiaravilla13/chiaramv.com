Back to the ’90s (and not only)

Lately it’s hard not to notice: retro packs, ads that quote other ads, jingles that trigger a chorus. And no — it’s not just aesthetics. It’s a cultural response.

Why now

Many describe this decade as “uneasy”: uncertainty isn’t only economic — it’s political, technological, social. When the present is noisy and the future is blurry, the past becomes precious because it’s already decoded.

It’s like taking a shortcut in a city you don’t know: it may not be the most scenic route, but it gets you there faster (and with less anxiety).

How it really works

Nostalgia marketing works because it activates familiarity. And familiarity reduces effort: you understand faster, you trust sooner, you “warm up” more quickly.

The past becomes a repertoire of ready-made meanings: you don’t have to explain everything from scratch. It’s an emotional remote control with a very convenient button: play.

Does it always work? Spoiler: no.

It works best when it taps into shared memories, not just individual taste. A generic “’90s texture” isn’t enough: it needs a reference many people recognise in the same way (an icon, a ritual, a place, a category code).

It’s not universal: it changes by generation, culture, context. What feels like “home” for some is just decoration for others.

Why we fall for it

A useful lens: nostalgia offers psychological safety. Not because “it used to be better”, but because “it used to be more predictable”.

It can trigger comfort, connection, a sense of control — sometimes even hope. (The best nostalgia isn’t melancholic: it’s reassuring.)

When it becomes just vintage

Here’s the risk: nostalgia can be a bridge… or a costume. It becomes “just vintage” when:

  • it’s pasted onto the brand without a brand logic
  • it replaces proof (process, standards, experience quality)
  • it distracts from something new that should be understood now (because it pulls you into the already-known)

In practice: three smart ways to use it

1

Heritage for legitimacy

To reinforce primacy, originality, continuity.

2

Nostalgia that energises

To trigger action: not only “those were the days”, but “ok, what do we do now?”.

3

Critical nostalgia

To correct or expand what was missing: not a replica, but a bridge to the present.

Takeaway

Nostalgia works when it’s a bridge (memory → present) and when it doesn’t replace proof. If it becomes pure aesthetics, it may warm up — but it won’t sustain.
