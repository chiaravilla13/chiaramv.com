Existing category or new market? The decision before positioning

“We have no competitors.” Sometimes it is a very strong statement. Other times, it is a warning sign. Because having no competitors can mean two very different things: you may have found an underserved space, or people may not yet understand where to place you.

The decision before positioning

This is where an important brand decision begins: is it better to enter an existing category, or try to build a new frame?

The answer is not automatic. It depends on how much your audience already understands the problem, how used they are to looking for a solution, how willing they are to change language, and how much energy you have to educate the market before you even sell.

Creating a new market can be powerful. But it is not free.

An existing category gives readability

Entering an already recognised category means relying on a map people already know. If you say “project management software”, “architecture studio”, “nutrition consulting”, “brand strategy”, people do not understand everything, but they understand where to place you.

They already have some expectations. They know which alternatives to consider. They know which questions to ask. They know, at least partly, what choosing you might mean.

This does not mean being the same as everyone else. It means using enough category codes so that people do not have to start from zero.

The category orients

It helps people understand where to place you.

The category creates expectations

It gives a first mental map of what you do.

The category is not enough

It makes you understandable, not automatically desirable.

The risk, of course, is becoming too similar to others. If you use all the same codes, words, promises and structures, you become readable but not very memorable.

A new market gives space, but requires more energy

Trying to build a new market means shifting the frame. You are not only saying: “choose me instead of my competitors”. You are saying: “look at the problem in a different way”.

This can be a very strong choice when the existing market is saturated, when the alternatives are all built on the same logic, or when the real value of your offer is not understood if you stay inside a traditional category.

But the price is higher. If people do not yet have a mental label for you, you need to build it. If they do not know which criteria to use to evaluate you, you need to suggest them. If they do not yet perceive the problem as a priority, you need to make it visible.

Before convincing people that you are the right choice, you need to convince them that the new frame makes sense.

“No competitors” is not enough

One of the most common mistakes is confusing the absence of declared competitors with a strategic advantage. But people always compare.

Even when there is no direct competitor, there are perceived alternatives. People can choose to do nothing. They can use a spreadsheet. They can rely on an internal solution. They can stay with the old provider. They can postpone. They can spend that budget elsewhere.

A competitor is not always someone who does exactly the same thing. Often, it is whatever already occupies space in the person’s mind, time, habits or budget.

That is why “we have no competitors” should be treated carefully. The more useful question is: compared to what should someone choose you?

The difference between being distinctive and becoming indecipherable

Every brand wants to differentiate. But not everything that is different helps people choose.

Sometimes, because they are afraid of sounding “like everyone else”, brands remove too many references: they change language, rename the category, avoid understandable words, and build a promise so original it becomes tiring.

The result is a paradox: the brand is different, but people do not know what to do with it.

A good brand decision is not “be as different as possible”. It is deciding where to be familiar and where to be new. Familiar enough to be understood. New enough to be remembered.

When to stay inside a category

Staying inside an existing category can be a good choice when the problem is already clear, the audience is already looking for solutions, and the main job is to make it clear why your offer is the better fit.

In this case, you do not necessarily need to reinvent the market. You need to clarify:

  • who you are the right choice for
  • which problem you solve better
  • which decision criterion you want to make more important
  • which proof supports your promise
  • which part of the category you interpret differently

Sometimes you do not need to create a new category. You just need to change the decision criterion: not “I am one alternative among many”, but “I am the most sensible alternative if this matters to you”.

When to shift the frame

Shifting the frame can make sense when the existing category compresses you. It happens when people evaluate you with the wrong criteria, compare you with alternatives that do not really solve the same problem, or cannot see your value inside the available labels.

In these cases, the work is deeper: not only explaining why people should choose you, but explaining why the problem should be seen in another way.

Clarity

Do not lose people while changing the frame.

Strength

Change the way they interpret the situation.

Proof

Language, examples, cases, comparisons and coherent touchpoints.

Inventing a new name is not enough. You need to build language, examples, cases, comparisons, educational content, criteria and coherent touchpoints.

The decision mini-matrix

Before deciding whether to enter an existing category or create a new frame, you can use this matrix.

  1. Is the problem already recognised?
    If yes, you can rely on the category and differentiate through criteria. If not, you first need to make the problem visible.
  2. Do people already know how to look for a solution?
    If yes, your job is to stand out in comparison. If not, your job is to educate before selling.
  3. Are there strong perceived alternatives?
    If yes, you need to clarify what you are different from. If not, you need to explain why the topic deserves attention.
  4. Does the current category help or limit you?
    If it helps, use it. If it limits you, shift the frame carefully.
  5. Do you have enough resources to educate the market?
    Creating a new category requires continuity. If you cannot sustain it, you risk staying interesting but not chosen.

In short

The choice is not between “being like everyone else” and “inventing a whole new world”. The most useful choice often sits in the middle.

  • Use the category to be understood.
  • Shift some criteria to be chosen.
  • Build distinctive signals to be remembered.

A strong brand does not necessarily need to create a new category. It needs to create a clear position in the mind of the right people.

The real goal is not to look like you have no competitors. It is to be understood, chosen and remembered for a precise reason.
